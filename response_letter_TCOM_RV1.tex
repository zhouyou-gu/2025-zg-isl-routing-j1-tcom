\section*{\centering\Large\textbf{Response to Reviewer 1 of Manuscript TCOM-TPS-26-0080}}

\textbf{Summary by Reviewer 1: This paper proposes a Lagrangian duality-based method, called DuJo, for the joint optimization of Laser Inter-Satellite Link (LISL) matching and traffic flow routing in Low Earth Orbit (LEO) mega constellations. The objective is to maximize network throughput while considering the mechanical constraints of Laser Communication Terminals (LCTs), such as steering ranges and pointing jitters, alongside non-uniform global traffic demands. However, the paper may not be suitable for publication in its current form. To improve the quality of the paper, the authors should consider the following comments:}


\textbf{Comment 1: Is the assumption of independent and identically distributed pointing errors across LCTs realistic in practical satellite platforms?}

\textbf{Response:} We thank the reviewer for this important comment. We would like to clarify that the i.i.d. Rayleigh assumption \cite{arnon2003effects} plays a limited technical role in our formulation, so the realism concern is narrower than it may appear. Specifically, the independence part is never actually invoked by the optimization: the per-link outage threshold $\hat{\nu}$ and the effective rate $r_{n,m}$ are derived purely from the marginal jitter distribution of a single link, and we never evaluate any joint outage event across multiple links. Hence correlated jitter across terminals would not alter any individual link's rate model nor any step of the matching/routing algorithm; it would matter only in a joint-outage analysis, which is outside our scope. The identically-distributed part is used only to adopt a common Rayleigh scale $\sigma_{\mathrm{J}}$, which is consistent with LCTs sharing the same mechanical design, control loop, and attitude reference on a common spacecraft bus after calibration \cite{kaymak2018survey,yang2024analysis}. Allowing per-terminal jitter scales would simply change the per-link marginal distribution and hence the values of $r_{n,m}$, leaving the matching/routing framework unchanged.

\textbf{Manuscript changes:} We have added a short remark to the jitter model in the system model section as follows:

``\dots\blue{Note that the i.i.d. assumption plays a limited role here: the subsequent per-link capacity derivation depends only on the marginal jitter distribution of a single link, so correlated jitter across terminals would not alter any link's capacity or the matching/routing algorithm \cite{kaymak2018survey,yang2024analysis}. Allowing heterogeneous per-terminal jitter scales would simply yield heterogeneous link rates without changing the framework.}''

\textbf{Comment 2: Why is the acquisition and tracking time loss strictly assumed to be zero? To the best of my knowledge, LCT alignment in practical satellite systems incurs non-negligible time overhead.}

\textbf{Response:} We agree with the reviewer that zero acquisition and tracking loss is not physically realistic for operational optical inter-satellite links \cite{kaymak2018survey,li2011analytical,scheinfeild2000acquisition}. Our intended scope is per-snapshot joint design, where the LCT matching, traffic routing, and rate allocation are optimized at a single time instant $t$; we do not model the ATP overhead incurred when the matching changes between consecutive snapshots. We acknowledge this as a genuine scope limitation: at short snapshot intervals on the order of seconds (as in our simulations), ATP overhead is not negligible compared to the snapshot duration, and the present formulation is appropriate only when ATP is assumed to be handled externally or when the snapshot can be treated as long enough to amortize setup time.

We further note that the current Lagrangian-dual decomposition naturally accommodates an ATP-aware extension. Specifically, on a discrete-time grid with snapshot index $\ell$, the ATP-aware problem can be cast as an MDP on the matching state, with the previous matching $\mathbf{c}_{\ell-1}$ and per-link ATP countdowns $\mathbf{h}_\ell$ included in the state, newly-established links incurring zero rate during their ATP period, and the reward being the per-snapshot throughput. Because the link-rate coupling constraint \eqref{eq:const:path:flow_rate} is still per-snapshot in this MDP, lifting it with per-snapshot multipliers $\lambda_\ell$ decomposes the Bellman operator into the same three subproblems as in our current formulation. The temporal coupling is absorbed only into the matching subproblem (a), which becomes an MDP-matching policy with Lagrangian-weighted per-step rewards and a continuation value, while the routing subproblem (b) and the rate-allocation subproblem (c) remain memoryless per-snapshot LPs. The spatial Lagrangian decomposition (current paper) and the temporal MDP structure (ATP) are therefore orthogonal, and our current formulation is recovered as the degenerate case with zero ATP time. We have revised the system model section to state this per-snapshot scope explicitly, acknowledge the ATP limitation, and outline the MDP extension, with full details provided in a new appendix.

\textbf{Manuscript changes:} We have replaced the ``ideal acquisition and tracking'' wording with an explicit per-snapshot scope, an ATP limitation statement, and the MDP extension as follows:

``\dots\blue{Our formulation performs per-snapshot joint design of the matching, routing, and rate allocation at a fixed time $t$, and does not model acquisition, pointing, and tracking (ATP) overheads that arise when the matching changes across snapshots \cite{kaymak2018survey,li2011analytical,scheinfeild2000acquisition}. At short snapshot intervals on the order of seconds, ATP overheads are not negligible, which we acknowledge as a scope limitation of this work. An ATP-aware extension can be cast as a Markov decision process (MDP) on the matching state in which our current three-way Lagrangian decomposition is preserved at each snapshot; details are provided in the appendix. The present formulation corresponds to the degenerate case with zero ATP time, and the full MDP extension is left as future work.}''

We have also added a new appendix ``An MDP Extension for Future Work'' that defines the MDP state/action/transition/reward, derives the per-snapshot Bellman operator under Lagrangian relaxation, and shows that routing (b) and rate allocation (c) remain per-snapshot LPs while only the matching subproblem (a) becomes an MDP-matching policy with Lagrangian-weighted rewards and a continuation value. The appendix also notes that approximate dynamic programming, deep reinforcement learning, and graph representation learning are natural candidates for approximating the value function, and recovers the present paper as the degenerate ATP-free case.

\textbf{Comment 3: You’d better provide a step-by-step mathematical reduction for the NP hardness proof.}

\textbf{Response:} We thank the reviewer for this comment, and the reviewer was right that the original NP-hardness discussion was too terse to support the claim rigorously. In the revised manuscript, we now discuss the complexity through the fixed-snapshot abstract decision problem, abstract snapshot joint matching and routing (AS-JMR), which is obtained by fixing one snapshot and abstracting away the geometric/orbital derivation of the connectable pairs, the source-target pair set, and the link rates. For that reason, we stated Proposition \ref{prop:np_hardness} for AS-JMR rather than directly for the full physical formulation \eqref{eq:prob:constrainted_routing_and_matching:primal}. 
The reason, stated explicitly in the remark after Proposition \ref{prop:np_hardness}, is that the current reduction fixes the combinatorial snapshot input and proves hardness for the resulting fixed-snapshot abstract decision problem, rather than deriving those objects from the underlying geometry, FOR constraints, distance thresholds, and traffic-pair construction rules. Therefore, NP-hardness of AS-JMR does not by itself yield a direct NP-hardness proof for the original geometric/orbital formulation. A direct NP-hardness claim for \eqref{eq:prob:constrainted_routing_and_matching:primal} would require an additional realization argument or a separate reduction that maps source instances to legal geometric/orbital instances of \eqref{eq:prob:constrainted_routing_and_matching:primal}. Depending on the geometric/orbital parameters, it is possible that some instances of \eqref{eq:prob:constrainted_routing_and_matching:primal} are not NP-hard, e.g., when there are no connectable pairs or no source-target pairs.
The appendix now gives a direct reduction from integral 2-commodity flow in symmetric digraphs \cite[Theorem 1]{jarry2012multiflows} to AS-JMR, with the construction and equivalence argument written step by step. This keeps the proposition, the scope clarification in the main text, and the appendix proof aligned.

\textbf{Comment 4: Will the subgradient descent handle potential oscillations when multiple flows compete for the same bottleneck LISLs?}

\textbf{Response:} We agree that subgradient ascent does not guarantee monotone per-iteration behavior when several flows compete for the same bottleneck LISLs. The mathematically correct guarantee is instead on the best-so-far dual value under diminishing step sizes \cite{boyd2003subgradient,boyd2004convex}. Transient oscillations can still occur, but their amplitude is damped asymptotically. We therefore revised the manuscript to state this distinction explicitly, rather than leaving the impression that every iterate is oscillation-free. We further revised the simulation discussion so that it no longer suggests monotone iteration-by-iteration improvement, and both the dual values and the converted throughput may fluctuate when bottleneck links are contested, but these fluctuations become milder as the step size decays.

\textbf{Manuscript changes:} We revised the relevant discussion as follows:

In the subgradient-descent description, we stated:

``\dots\blue{Because several flows may compete for the same bottleneck LISLs, the subgradient iterates need not be monotone: the dual variables and the converted primal solutions can oscillate before settling. The diminishing step size attenuates these oscillations asymptotically, so the convergence guarantee below is stated for the best-so-far dual iterate rather than for every individual iterate.}''

In Theorem \ref{theorem:convergence:classic}, we stated:
